How Setback actually works

Last updated: draft

Setback answers "which permits does my project need, and what usually goes wrong here" by combining three layers of information — and it's worth knowing which layer is behind any given claim, since they carry different weight.

1. Public record

County and municipal ordinances, agency names, and permit fee schedules — the baseline any roadmap starts from. This is publicly available information, compiled so you don't have to track it down yourself across a dozen different government sites.

2. Patterns from real pulls

Aggregate patterns rolled up from roadmaps Setback has actually generated — for example, which flags come up most often for a given trade in a given county. This is never a reprint of any one customer's specific paid output; it's a rollup across many.

3. Outcomes reported by other users

After a permit is decided, Setback asks what happened — approved as drafted, approved after comments, or rejected. Enough of these reports for a given county turn into real numbers: what share of projects like yours actually cleared review without changes. This is the layer that improves the most over time, and the one no generic AI assistant has access to.

Until a county has enough reported outcomes, its roadmap says so directly rather than showing a number — "not enough reported outcomes yet," not a guess dressed up as a statistic.

What this doesn't mean

None of the above is a live connection to any government system, and none of it substitutes for confirming current requirements with the actual issuing agency before you submit or build. See the disclaimer for the specifics.

Why report your outcome

Every real report makes the next roadmap for someone building in your county a little sharper — and it's the only reason Setback gets better at all, rather than just staying a well-organized version of public information forever.
