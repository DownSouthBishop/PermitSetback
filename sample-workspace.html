Before you get to permits

Zoning Setback Requirements — high impact
Portland's residential zones (R1–R20) have specific rear and side yard setbacks that vary by zone. A new 200 sq ft footprint could encroach depending on existing lot coverage and proximity to property lines.
Lot Coverage Limits — high impact
Portland zones impose maximum lot coverage ratios. Adding 200 sq ft of covered structure could push total coverage over the allowed percentage on smaller urban lots.
Historic District or Conservation Overlay — high impact
Portland has numerous historic districts (Irvington, Ladd's Addition, Eastmoreland). If the property is in one, Historic Landmarks Commission approval may be required before any exterior addition.
Title 11 Tree Protections — medium impact
Portland's Urban Forestry regulations protect significant trees (12"+ DBH). Construction could require a tree permit or protection plan if regulated trees are near the build zone.
Stormwater / Impervious Surface — medium impact
A roofed screened porch adds impervious coverage and could require on-site stormwater management measures under Bureau of Environmental Services rules.

Every agency you'll answer to

Portland Bureau of Development Services (BDS) — Building Permit

Primary permit authority for all residential structural additions. Required for any new covered structure attached to a home; plans must show framing, foundation/footing details, egress, and electrical if applicable.

Portland BDS — Zoning / Land Use Review

Verifies the porch complies with setbacks, lot coverage maximums, and height limits for the applicable residential zone. Screened porches count toward lot coverage.

Portland Fire Bureau — Life Safety Review

Fire separation and egress path from the addition checked as part of BDS routing, not a separate application in most cases.

Portland Bureau of Environmental Services (BES) — Stormwater

A screened porch with a solid roof adds impervious area; BES review or a stormwater management plan may be required.

Oregon Residential Specialty Code (ORSC) — Structural Compliance

Plans must meet ORSC requirements for footings, framing, connections, and seismic bracing — reviewed by BDS plan examiners.

The rules that trip up projects like this

Portland residential zones impose lot coverage limits (commonly 50% in R2/R5 zones) — screened porches with solid roofs typically count fully toward lot coverage.
Rear setbacks are commonly 15–20 ft and side setbacks 5 ft in Portland R zones, but your specific zone controls — verify before finalizing porch placement.
Oregon is in a high seismic zone. The ORSC requires prescriptive lateral bracing and positive connections at footing-to-post and post-to-beam for any new structure.
